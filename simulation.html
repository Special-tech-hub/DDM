What-If Analysis

WiFi Intervention
Simulation Model

What happens to student digital maturity levels if campus Wi-Fi reliability is improved to 99% uptime?

The Simulation Question

We selectively boost the Wi-Fi reliability scores for students who rated it poorly and re-run the Random Forest classifier. The goal is to observe how many students shift between maturity clusters — Emerging, Developing, Established, Leading — compared to the baseline distribution.

This helps us understand the impact of targeted infrastructure improvements on overall digital maturity at UZ.

4-Cluster Maturity Model

For the simulation, K-Means is run with k=4 to produce a richer maturity spectrum.

Emerging

Low digital adoption. Struggles with all four dimensions. Needs foundational support.

Developing

Partial adoption. Some dimensions are improving but WiFi and Emhare remain weak.

Established

Comfortable with digital services. Moderate scores across all dimensions.

Leading

High digital maturity. Confident, efficient, and satisfied across all dimensions.

Cluster Shift Analysis

16 out of 32 test-set students changed clusters after the WiFi intervention.

From (Baseline)To (After WiFi Boost)CountInterpretation
Leading Emerging 6 Leading students had high WiFi scores — boosting everyone's WiFi removes their differentiator
Established Emerging 5 Established students also relied on WiFi advantage; other dimensions are moderate
Developing Emerging 3 Developing students shift to Emerging when WiFi is equalised
Emerging Established 2 Some Emerging students benefit and move up when WiFi improves
Paradox Explained: When WiFi is boosted uniformly to 4.9/5, most students collapse into the Emerging cluster. This is because WiFi was the primary differentiator — students who previously scored high on WiFi (Leading/Established) now have the same score as everyone else. The classifier then relies on the remaining dimensions (Google Classroom, Clearance, Emhare), which are all moderate (~3.0) across all students. This confirms that WiFi improvement alone is not sufficient — all four dimensions must be developed together to achieve higher maturity levels.

Simulation Conclusions

1

WiFi alone is not enough

Boosting WiFi uniformly collapses most students into the Emerging cluster because the other dimensions remain underdeveloped. A holistic approach is required.

2

Google Classroom is the second lever

In the 4-cluster model, Google Classroom confidence (29%) is the second most important feature. Training programmes will have significant impact on maturity classification.

3

Targeted intervention beats uniform improvement

Boosting WiFi only for students who rated it poorly (≤2) would be more effective than a uniform boost — it lifts the floor without disrupting the existing differentiation.

4

Parallel investment is the optimal strategy

Invest in WiFi infrastructure AND Google Classroom training AND clearance automation simultaneously. Sequential investment will not achieve the 4.0 target by Q4.
